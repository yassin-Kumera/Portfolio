The short version

I hold an MA in Education specialising in special needs, and I spent six years founding and running an inclusive early-years centre in The Gambia, designing individual learning for children with autism and other additional needs, and training the staff around them to do the same.

That frontline work led me to build Sunu Baat ("Our Voice"), a free communication app for non-verbal children, in Wolof and English, because almost no learning or communication tools reflect how children here actually speak and live.

Now I'm turning that same instinct toward learning design: making digital learning that is clear, accessible, and built for the people who are usually designed around, not for.

Selected work

Live product

Sunu Baat: "Our Voice"

A free AAC communication app for non-verbal children in Wolof and English. I ran the whole thing myself: needs analysis, design, build, and classroom testing, using AI and no-code tools. Includes a culturally-grounded symbol set and caregiver voice recording so a child can speak in a familiar voice.

The thinking behind it
The problem
Non-verbal children in The Gambia and Senegal had almost no way to communicate. Existing AAC apps are expensive, English-only, and built around symbols and situations that do not match how children here live.
How I knew
Seven years of frontline work with autistic children and their families, plus my own research interviews with mothers who described children who understood everything and could say nothing.
Design decisions
Wolof and English side by side, so a child is not forced into a second language to be understood. A caregiver voice-recording feature, because a familiar voice matters more than a synthetic one. A symbol set drawn from local food, family and routines rather than imported clip art. Free, because cost was one of the barriers in the first place.
Measuring it
Early piloting with families and a partner classroom, watching which symbols get used, what is missing, and whether children initiate communication rather than only responding. Feedback is shaping the next build.

Live course

Understanding Autism in the Mainstream Classroom

A short, self-paced module I designed and built in Articulate Rise for mainstream teachers, covering how autism can show up in class and the small adjustments that change a child's whole day. Includes a branching classroom scenario, flashcards and knowledge checks, with alt text and clear structure throughout.

The thinking behind it
The problem
Autistic children are placed in mainstream classrooms with teachers who have had no SEN training. The gap is rarely willingness, it is knowing what a behaviour means and what to do in the moment.
Who it is for
Mainstream primary teachers with no specialist background, who have fifteen minutes, not a training week.
Design decisions
Opened with a single classroom moment rather than a definition, so the learner starts from something they recognise. Chunked the content into four ways autism shows up, so it is scannable rather than encyclopaedic. Used a branching scenario for the core skill, because judgement in the moment is learned by practising it, not by reading about it. Framed every adjustment as "instead of this, try this", so a teacher leaves with actions, not theory. Kept it to fifteen minutes because that is what a teacher actually has.
Accessibility
Alt text throughout, meaningful heading structure, nothing depending on colour alone, and plain language. A module about inclusion should be usable by everyone it talks about.
Measuring it
Knowledge checks give in-module signal, but the real measure is behavioural: whether teachers name a specific adjustment they made afterwards. A short follow-up two weeks later asking what they changed would tell me far more than a completion rate.

Live work

Animated research stories

I turned findings from my MA research, interviews with mothers of autistic children in The Gambia, into short animated films, so lived experience could reach the people who need to hear it. Captioned, and made to travel: they've reached thousands of viewers and started real conversations among parents and educators.

The thinking behind it
The problem
My research findings sat in a dissertation almost nobody outside a university would read, while the parents living those findings had no way of knowing they were not alone.
The decision
Turn the interviews into short animated stories rather than a report. Animation let me protect the mothers' identities while keeping their words, and it travels in a way a PDF does not.
Design decisions
One story, one theme per film, so each is a complete thought. Captions throughout, because reach matters and so does access. Kept the mothers' own framing rather than translating it into academic language.
What happened
Thousands of views, and comments from parents and educators recognising themselves in the stories. That recognition was the point: research turned back toward the people it came from.
